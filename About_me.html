My story

How I Have Become a Techthusiast
Despite being an Asian, my math had been mediocre, English was lousy, and Chinese was enough for communication. In my childhood, all my friends owned a Nintendo DS, an old-fashioned game console. But I did not have one so I asked my parents to buy it for me. Here is the most frequent scenario: almost all Asian parents will not refuse to buy one for kids but want this to be a motivation for academics. My parents were like “If you score xx this term, you can have it on my word.” Obviously, it’s demotivating to me.

Not getting an NDS opened another door to me. I disassembled my mom’s desktops and my dad taught me components like CPU and RAM. I was fascinated by those beautiful designs. Ever since then, I have become a fan of computers or anything tech-related. I dreamt of myself being a CREATOR instead of a CONSUMER.

How An Exchange Changed My Life
My tech-savvy mindset did not fit well in high school. I was stifled by the academic work. In the Asian education system, everyone was expected to be a lawyer, doctor, or teacher; however, I always knew that I did not want to be another brick in the wall built for a factory. My aimlessness became inertia.

The year of 2016 was my turning point. I had an opportunity to do an exchange in Veneto, Italy — a place I would never dream of as my English was awful, let alone to learn Italian. But my second thought told me that if I don’t go, my regrets would last forever.

It turns out to be the best decision — my life became a fertile ground for my youthful exploration. My English level was as bad as my Italian classmates, and I did not understand Italian. So I was forced to learn Italian for survival. Literally, I had to learn “Where is the toilet?” in Italian. That immersion made me perceived that life outside of school, the maximum score was beyond 100. Nobody would cramp you with content. I must dabble around to find my own path. My mindset has changed: “Everything was learnable; it is a matter of time and asking the right questions.”


I then came back to Hong Kong to finish my dull high school education diploma. The world is too big to just stay in one place, so I decided to stay in Melbourne, Australia for my tertiary education. Youtube is life-changing to me as I learn almost everything there. I love going beyond what the curriculum taught me and doing projects. Research is my passion whether academic or not. It allows me to explore the gap between new and existing knowledge. It gave me another lens, typically a deeper one, to look at the world.
